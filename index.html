Founder's Story

From a kina spike to a kelp forest mission

I grew up diving in kelp forests so thick the fronds felt like sea snakes grabbing your leg. In 2021, I dove into a barren reef at Little Barrier Island to find it stripped bare — and a kina spike to the thumb put me in hospital two weeks later. That's where Kelp Helpers began.

The Problem

Kelp forests are collapsing worldwide

Kelp forests have declined across 43 countries, driven by a range of complex, often overlapping challenges:

  • Urchin barrens — overfishing removes natural predators, letting urchin populations explode and graze reefs down to bare rock.
  • Marine heatwaves — rising ocean temperatures push kelp beyond its thermal tolerance, causing large-scale die-offs.
  • Ocean acidification — changing ocean chemistry weakens kelp's ability to grow and recover.
  • Coastal pollution and runoff — sediment and nutrient pollution smother reefs and block the light kelp needs.
  • Coastal development — dredging, construction, and habitat loss strip away the reef structure kelp depends on.
  • Invasive species — introduced grazers and competitors outcompete native kelp in many regions.

Kelp Helpers is leveraging the best scalable innovations through collaboration — removing barriers, returning kelp, and building circular economies that make restoration self-funding.
